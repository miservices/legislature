How a Bill Becomes Law

Follow the journey of a bill as it moves through the Michigan Legislature—from introduction to enactment. Click each step to explore what happens along the way.

1

Introduction

A bill may be introduced in either chamber. Senate bills are filed with the Secretary of the Senate, and House bills with the Clerk of the House. Bills receive a number and are read by title.
2

Committee Referral & Review

The bill is referred to a standing committee, where members debate the bill, may hold public hearings, and decide whether to report the bill to the full chamber.
3

Second & Third Reading

The bill is considered by the full chamber. Amendments may be offered, debate occurs, and the bill advances to a final vote at Third Reading.
4

Vote in First Chamber

A roll call vote is taken. If a majority of members elected and serving vote in favor, the bill passes and moves to the other chamber.
5

Second Chamber Consideration

The second chamber follows the same committee and floor process. If the bill is amended, both chambers must agree to identical language.
6

Conference Committee (If Needed)

If the chambers disagree, a conference committee attempts to resolve differences. Both chambers must approve the final compromise.
7

Governor’s Action

The Governor may sign the bill, veto it, or allow it to become law without a signature. The Legislature may override a veto by a two-thirds vote.
8

Becomes Law

Once enacted, the law takes effect either after 90 days or immediately if granted immediate effect by a two-thirds vote of both chambers.
